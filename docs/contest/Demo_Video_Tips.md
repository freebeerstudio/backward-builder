# Demo Video Tips for Vibeathon Participants

Your demo video is one of the most important parts of your submission. Judges watch every video — it's your chance to show what you built, why it matters, and who you are as a team. These tips will help you make the most of it.

## Why the Video Matters

The video carries significant weight in your overall score. It's the judges' main window into whether your project actually works, whether you understand what you built, and whether the problem you're solving is real and meaningful.

A polished deck with no working demo is a red flag. A rough video showing a genuinely working product is a win.

Think of it like an investor pitch: investors sift through hundreds of proposals, and if what you built isn't instantly clear, you're forgotten in seconds. A good demo video compresses weeks of work into minutes, delivering clarity and impact that code alone can't.

## Think Like a Founder Pitching Investors

The best demo videos don't just show features — they tell a story. Investors and judges respond to the same things: a clear problem, a credible solution, and evidence that you're the right team to build it.

- **Lead with your strongest number.** If you have a metric — users served, time saved, problems solved during testing — put it in the first 10 seconds. Front-loading credibility grabs attention fast.
- **Tell a story, not a feature list.** Walk the judges through a journey: here's someone with this problem, here's what their life looks like before your app, and here's what happens when they use it.
- **Show the market, not just the product.** Even briefly: who needs this? How many of them are there? Is this a toy or a real business?
- **End with a vision.** What does this become if you keep building? What customer would pay for this tomorrow?
- **Skip the buzzwords.** The more you say "AI-powered" without showing what that means, the less credible you sound.

## What Judges Are Looking For

1. **Did you solve a real problem?** — Is the pain point obvious? Does your solution actually address it?
2. **Is the demo real?** — Real data, real interactions, real edge cases — not pre-loaded samples or hardcoded mockups.
3. **Do you understand what you built?** — Can you go off-script? Do you explain why you made technical choices?
4. **Is it usable?** — Can a new user figure out what to do within seconds?
5. **Did you ship something complete?** — 3 features done well beats 10 features half-done.

## Structure Your Video (3–5 minutes)

### 1. Hook (0:00 – 0:30)
Start with the problem, not the product. Don't say "We built an app called X." Say: "Every small business owner wastes 3 hours a week on [painful thing]. We built something that eliminates that."

One sentence on the pain. One sentence on your solution. Then start the demo.

### 2. Live Demo (0:30 – 3:30)
This is the most important part. Show the app working end-to-end:

- Start from a blank state — log in fresh, create something new
- Narrate decisions, not clicks — "I'm clicking here because..." not "Now I click the button"
- Show the core loop — the thing your app actually does, once completely, clearly
- Trigger an edge case — an error message that handles gracefully, a validation that catches bad input

### 3. Technical Walk (3:30 – 4:30) *(optional but powerful)*
A 60-second tour of one technically interesting part of your codebase. Not a GitHub README tour — something like: "Here's how we handle the AI response parsing, because the raw output needed..."

### 4. Close (4:30 – 5:00)
End with a forward-looking statement. What would you build next? What customer would use this first? Keep it brief and genuine.

## Do This

- **Show real data.** Type something new live. Submit a real form. Don't narrate over pre-loaded sample data.
- **Explain the AI integration.** How: multi-step prompting? structured output? fallback handling? "We called the OpenAI API" is table stakes. "We built a retry loop that degrades gracefully when the model times out" is impressive.
- **Prepare your environment.** Close Slack, hide notifications, mute alerts. Clean browser tab. Localhost warmed up.
- **Use your whole team.** Two people demoing is more interesting than one.
- **Narrate with context.** "This matters because..." and "We chose this approach because..."
- **Show what you're proud of.** If you built something clever, show it and explain why.
- **Add captions.** Many judges will skim on mute first.
- **Write a quick script.** ~450 words for 3 minutes.

## Avoid This

- **Mismatch between demo and code.** Biggest trust-breaker.
- **Narrating a UI with no data.** Empty screens or pre-staged data that you clearly set up in advance.
- **Reading bullet points.** Talk about what you built like you understand it.
- **"AI-powered" without depth.** Show the sophistication: prompt structure, bad output handling, model failure behavior.
- **Rushing through unfinished features.** Better to say "we ran out of time on X" than demo something broken.
- **Too long.** If you haven't made your point in 5 minutes, extra time hurts.

## Recording Tips

- **Resolution:** 1080p or higher. Zoom in for small elements.
- **Audio:** Use a headset or sit close to a microphone.
- **File format:** MP4 is ideal. Platform accepts MP4, MOV, WebM, and AVI up to 100MB.
- **Test recording:** 30 seconds first to check audio/video.
- **Don't over-edit.** One clean take is better than choppy edits. Minor mistakes show it's real.

## Uploading Your Video

Submit through the vibeathon platform (no YouTube links). Video upload becomes available 15 minutes after your code is submitted. Submit code first, then video.

## One Last Thing

The judges are rooting for you. A genuine demo of something real — even if imperfect — beats a polished presentation of something fake. Show what you actually built, explain why you built it, and let the work speak for itself.

Good luck.
